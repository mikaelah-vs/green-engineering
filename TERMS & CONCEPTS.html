Terms & Concepts

Green Infrastructure

Definition of Terms

Nature-Based Solutions: An engineering approach that utilizes natural elements (soil, plants, hydrology) rather than strictly synthetic materials like concrete and steel to solve infrastructure challenges.

The "Sponge" Concept: A design philosophy where a site is engineered to absorb and hold water locally, rather than acting as a "slide" that sheds water immediately into a sewer system.

Co-benefits: Secondary positive outcomes of a project that go beyond its primary engineering function. For example, a drainage system that also improves air quality and creates community green space.

Detention and Infiltration: The strategic slowing down (detention) and soaking into the ground (infiltration) of stormwater, as opposed to high-velocity conveyance through pipes.

Peak Flow: The maximum rate of water runoff during a storm event. Green infrastructure aims to reduce this peak to prevent overwhelming municipal systems.

Physical Engineering Features

Bioswales: Engineered depressions filled with specialized soil and vegetation. They replace traditional curbs and gutters to slow, filter, and sink runoff.

Permeable Pavement: A porous alternative to standard asphalt or concrete that allows water to pass through into an underlying structural stone reservoir for temporary storage.

Green Roofs: Rooftop systems that use soil and plants to manage water. They provide the added benefit of protecting waterproof membranes from UV damage and thermal expansion.

Grey Engineering: The traditional infrastructure model relying on "hard" materials like concrete, steel, and underground pipes to manage water as a waste product.

Biological & Hydrologic Processes

Evapotranspiration: The process by which water is transferred from the land to the atmosphere by evaporation from the soil and by transpiration from plants.

Infiltration: The process by which water on the ground surface enters the soil; a key goal of mimicking the natural water cycle in urban centers.

Specialized Soil Media: Engineered soil mixes used in bioswales to ensure high filtration rates while supporting the growth of salt-tolerant vegetation.

Natural Hydrologic Processes: The pathways through which water moves in nature (absorption, filtration, evaporation) which green infrastructure seeks to replicate in the built environment.

Asset Management

Living Assets: Infrastructure components (like plants and soil) that require active maintenance—such as weeding and health checks—to remain functional, unlike passive "buried" assets.

Climate-Resilient Footprint: An urban design that is capable of withstanding environmental stressors (like heavy rain or heat) by working in harmony with natural systems.

Key Components

Permeable Pavement: Funtion - Infiltration & Sub-surface storage; Practical Benefit - Reduces need for detention ponds.

Bioswales: Function - Filtration & Flow attenuation; Practical Benefit - Removes heavy metals and oils from runoff.

Green Roofs: Function - Evapotranspiration & Insulation; Practical Benefit - Lowers building energy costs and "Heat Island" effect.
